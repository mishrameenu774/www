Composting

Composting is the process of breaking down organic waste, such as food scraps, yard trimmings, and other biodegradable materials, into nutrient-rich soil or humus. This decomposition is carried out by microorganisms, including bacteria, fungi, and insects, which work to break down the material over time. The process occurs in a compost bin or pile, where the organic matter is kept in a controlled environment to ensure efficient decomposition. Composting helps reduce the amount of waste sent to landfills, enriches soil, improves plant growth, and reduces the need for chemical fertilizers. The most common method of composting is aerobic, which requires regular turning of the compost to ensure it gets enough oxygen for microorganisms to thrive.

Need of Composting

1. Reduces Waste: Composting keeps biodegradable waste out of landfills, reducing methane emissions and pollution.
2. Enriches Soil: Compost enhances soil fertility, supports plant growth, and improves water retention.
3. Closes the Loop: It’s a sustainable way to recycle organic waste into a useful resource for home and community gardens.

How to use

1. In Gardens: Mix compost into your garden soil to enhance its fertility and water-holding capacity. Spread it around plants as mulch to suppress weeds and retain moisture.
2. For Potted Plants: Add a thin layer of compost to the topsoil in pots to provide plants with nutrients. Mix compost with regular soil for a balanced growing medium.
3. Lawn Care: Sprinkle compost over your lawn to improve soil health and promote lush, green grass.
4. Compost Tea: Soak a bag of compost in water for a few days to create a nutrient-rich liquid fertilizer. Use this to water plants.

Composting Process

Promoting home and community composting starts with understanding the process. Here’s a simple guide:
1. Collect Organic Waste: Combine greens (nitrogen sources like fruit peels) and browns (carbon sources like dry leaves).
2. Layer in a Bin: Set up a compost bin with alternating layers of greens and browns (2:1 ratio).
3. Maintain: Turn the pile regularly and keep it moist but not soggy.
4. Wait: Decomposition takes 2–6 months. Finished compost looks dark, crumbly, and smells earthy.

Result

Finished compost is:
- Dark and Crumbly: Looks like rich, fertile soil.
- Nutrient-rich: Packed with essential nutrients for plants.
- Earthy Smelling: Free of bad odors, indicating it’s fully decomposed.
By using compost in urban gardens and landscapes, we create greener spaces, reduce landfill dependency, and contribute to a cleaner, more sustainable future.

TIPS

Here are some composting tips: 1. Balance Green and Brown Materials: Use a 2:1 ratio of brown (carbon-rich) to green (nitrogen-rich) materials.
2. Chop and Shred: Break large items into smaller pieces for quicker decomposition.
3. Turn Regularly: Aerate by turning the compost every few weeks to promote airflow and prevent odors.
4. Maintain Moisture: Keep compost damp, like a wrung-out sponge, to support microorganisms.
5. Avoid Dairy, Meat, and Oils: These can attract pests and slow down the process.
6. Add Worms: For vermicomposting, worms can accelerate decomposition and improve compost quality.
7. Use a Bin or Pile: Keep compost contained to stay organized and tidy.
8. Monitor Temperature: Maintain a temperature of 130-160°F (54-71°C) for optimal decomposition.
